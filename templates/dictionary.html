Toxin Types

Learn about different types of toxins found in Australian wildlife.

Neurotoxins

Definition: Toxins that damage or disrupt the functioning of nerve tissue.

Effects: Can cause paralysis, seizures, altered nerve function, and disrupted communication between nerves and muscles.

Examples: Found in many Australian elapid snakes (e.g., Eastern Brown Snake), funnel-web spiders, blue-ringed octopus (tetrodotoxin).

Hemotoxins (Haemotoxins)

Definition: Toxins that destroy red blood cells, disrupt blood clotting, and cause tissue damage.

Effects: Can cause internal bleeding, organ damage, and disruption of the circulatory system.

Examples: Found in some Australian snake venoms, particularly Tiger Snakes and Brown Snakes.

Cytotoxins

Definition: Toxins that cause cell death and tissue destruction.

Effects: Can cause necrosis, tissue damage, and localized cell destruction.

Examples: Found in jellyfish stings, some spider venom, and certain snake species.

Myotoxins

Definition: Toxins that specifically damage muscle tissue.

Effects: Can cause muscle pain, weakness, damage, and in severe cases, rhabdomyolysis (breakdown of skeletal muscle).

Examples: Found in the venom of Tiger Snakes and some other Australian elapids.

Medical Terms

Key medical terminology related to venom and toxin effects.

Coagulopathy

Definition: A condition in which the blood's ability to clot is impaired.

Effects: Can lead to excessive bleeding, internal hemorrhaging, and complications from blood loss.

Related to: Many Australian snake venoms, including those from Brown Snakes and Tiger Snakes.

Coagulate

Definition: The process by which blood changes from a liquid to a gel or solid state, forming a clot.

Function: Normal coagulation stops excessive bleeding when a blood vessel is injured.

Impact of venoms: Some venoms can prevent coagulation (anticoagulants), while others can cause abnormal clotting (procoagulants).

Envenomation

Definition: The process by which venom is injected into a person or animal by bite, sting, or other means.

Effects: Varies widely depending on the type of venom, amount injected, and location of injection.

Treatment: Depends on the type of envenomation but may include antivenom, pressure immobilization, and supportive care.

Necrosis

Definition: The death of body tissue due to lack of blood flow or serious injury, including from toxins.

Effects: Tissue turns black and dies, may require surgical removal in severe cases.

Related to: Certain spider bites (e.g., white-tailed spider, though this is contested), some snake bites, and marine creature injuries.

Treatment Terminology

Terms related to treating envenomation and toxin exposure.

Antivenom

Definition: A biological product used to treat venomous bites and stings, containing antibodies against specific venoms.

Function: Binds to and neutralizes venom components in the victim's body.

Types in Australia: Specific antivenoms exist for snakes (brown, tiger, black, taipan, death adder), funnel-web spiders, redback spiders, box jellyfish, and other species.

Pressure Immobilization Technique

Definition: A first aid technique used for venomous bites and stings to slow the movement of venom through the body.

Method: A firm bandage is applied over the bite site and along the affected limb, which is then immobilized with a splint.

Uses: Recommended for bites from most Australian venomous snakes, funnel-web spiders, and blue-ringed octopuses.
